Breast Cancer Information

Symptoms

  • New lump in breast or underarm
  • Thickening or swelling of breast tissue
  • Irritation or dimpling of breast skin
  • Nipple discharge other than milk
  • Pain in any area of the breast
  • Change in breast size or shape
  • Redness or flaky skin in nipple area

Precautions & Prevention

  • Monthly self-examinations after age 20
  • Clinical breast exams every 1-3 years (20-39)
  • Annual mammograms after age 40
  • Maintain healthy weight and exercise
  • Limit alcohol consumption to 1 drink/day
  • Consider genetic testing if family history
  • Breastfeed if possible

Advice for Affected Individuals

  • Create a support network of family and friends
  • Discuss all treatment options with oncologist
  • Consider joining support groups
  • Manage treatment side effects proactively
  • Maintain nutrition and light exercise
  • Consider fertility preservation if needed
  • Address emotional health with counseling

Screening Recommendations

Women at average risk should begin yearly mammograms at age 40. Those with family history or genetic mutations may need earlier screening starting at age 30. Clinical breast exams are recommended every 1-3 years for women in their 20s-30s and annually after 40.

Prostate Cancer Information

Symptoms

  • Trouble urinating or weak flow
  • Blood in urine or semen
  • Persistent pain in back/hips/pelvis
  • Erectile dysfunction
  • Discomfort when sitting (enlarged prostate)
  • Frequent urination, especially at night
  • Pain or burning during urination

Precautions & Prevention

  • PSA blood test discussion at age 50 (45 for high-risk)
  • Digital rectal exam (DRE) as recommended
  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Regular exercise regimen
  • Increase consumption of tomatoes and cruciferous vegetables
  • Limit red meat and high-fat dairy
  • Consider selenium and vitamin E supplements

Advice for Affected Individuals

  • Understand Gleason score and cancer stage
  • Discuss active surveillance vs. treatment options
  • Consider side effects of treatments (incontinence, ED)
  • Explore support groups for men
  • Maintain pelvic floor exercises
  • Address mental health concerns
  • Consider impact on sexual health and relationships

Screening Recommendations

Men should discuss prostate screening with their doctor starting at age 50 (age 45 for African Americans or those with family history). Screening includes PSA blood test and may include digital rectal exam. Screening frequency depends on PSA levels and risk factors.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the survival rate for early-stage breast cancer?

The 5-year survival rate for localized breast cancer is 99%. Early detection through screening dramatically improves outcomes. Survival rates decrease as the cancer progresses to more advanced stages.

Are prostate cancer screenings painful?

The PSA test is a simple blood test. The digital rectal exam (DRE) may cause brief discomfort but is not typically painful. Both are quick procedures that can be completed during a routine doctor's visit.

Can men get breast cancer?

Yes, though rare (about 1% of all breast cancers). Men should report any breast lumps or changes to their doctor immediately. Risk factors include family history, radiation exposure, and certain genetic conditions.

What are the treatment options for prostate cancer?

Options include active surveillance, surgery (prostatectomy), radiation therapy, hormone therapy, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and targeted therapy. Treatment choice depends on cancer stage, age, and overall health.

Are cancer medications covered by insurance?

Most insurance plans cover FDA-approved cancer medications, but copays vary. Many pharmaceutical companies offer patient assistance programs. Our pharmacists can help you navigate coverage options and financial assistance.

How do I choose the right oncologist?

Consider the doctor's specialization, experience with your specific cancer type, hospital affiliation, communication style, and patient reviews. Many patients get second opinions before starting treatment.
